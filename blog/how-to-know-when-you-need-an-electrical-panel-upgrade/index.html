How to Know When You Need an Electrical Panel Upgrade

Your electrical panel is the nerve center of your home's power system. If it's struggling to keep up with today's demands, the signs can be subtle at first but grow into serious safety hazards.

Marcus Johnson Master Electrician, Johnson's Electric of Charlotte

What Does an Electrical Panel Actually Do?

Your electrical panel (also called a breaker box or load center) receives power from the utility company and distributes it throughout your home via individual circuit breakers. Each breaker protects a specific circuit from drawing too much current. When a circuit is overloaded, the breaker trips and cuts the power to prevent overheating and potential fires.

Most homes built before 1990 were designed with panels rated at 100 amps or 150 amps. Today, the average home uses significantly more electricity due to high-efficiency HVAC systems, electric vehicles, home offices, smart home devices, and large kitchen appliances. A panel that worked fine 30 years ago may now be chronically underpowered.

7 Warning Signs Your Panel Needs an Upgrade

Pay close attention to the following warning signs. Any one of them warrants a call to a licensed electrician for an inspection.

1. Breakers That Trip Frequently

An occasional tripped breaker is normal. If you are regularly resetting the same breaker or multiple breakers trip at the same time, your panel is telling you it can't handle the electrical load on those circuits. This is a strong signal that you need additional capacity.

2. Lights That Flicker or Dim

When you run a large appliance and notice lights dimming throughout the house, your panel is struggling to distribute power evenly. This is especially common when a dryer, HVAC unit, or air compressor starts up and draws a large surge of current.

3. You Have a Fuse Box Instead of Breakers

Homes with fuse boxes rather than circuit breakers are operating on technology from before the 1960s. Fuse boxes present real fire hazards because people often replace blown fuses with the wrong amperage. If your home still uses fuses, an upgrade is not optional, it is urgent.

4. A Burning Smell or Scorch Marks

Any burning odor near your panel, or visible scorch marks around outlets and breakers, indicates a serious problem. Do not delay. Turn off your main breaker if you smell burning and call an electrician immediately.

5. Your Panel Is a Known Problematic Brand

Certain panel brands manufactured from the 1950s through the 1990s are known to be defective. Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) Stab-Lok panels and Zinsco panels are two of the most notable. These brands have documented failure rates that make them a fire risk regardless of how old they are. If you have one of these panels, replacement is strongly recommended.

6. You Are Adding Major Appliances or an EV Charger

Planning to install a Level 2 EV charger, a new HVAC system, a hot tub, or a home addition? These all require dedicated circuits and significant amperage. Most require a panel upgrade to 200 amps before installation can proceed safely.

7. Your Panel Is 25 or More Years Old

Electrical panels are generally rated for a service life of 25 to 40 years. If your panel is approaching or past that range, have it inspected by a licensed electrician. Older panels may have worn components, loose connections, or outdated wiring that creates risk even if no obvious symptoms exist yet.

What Happens During a Panel Upgrade?

A panel upgrade involves replacing the existing breaker box with a new, higher-capacity unit. Here is what the process typically looks like when Johnson's Electric handles the job:

  • Initial assessment: We review your current panel, calculate your home's total electrical load, and determine the right panel size (typically 200 amps for most residential upgrades).
  • Permit and inspection: We pull the required permits through the city or county. All panel upgrades in Charlotte must be permitted and inspected by a licensed inspector.
  • Utility coordination: We coordinate with Duke Energy or your local utility to schedule a temporary service disconnect during installation.
  • Installation: The old panel is removed and the new panel is installed, with all existing circuits transferred and properly labeled.
  • Inspection and reconnect: After passing the electrical inspection, the utility reconnects service and your home is fully powered through the new panel.

The entire process typically takes one day. Your home will have a brief power outage during the swap, usually 4 to 6 hours.

How Much Does a Panel Upgrade Cost in Charlotte?

Panel upgrade costs in the Charlotte area typically range from $1,800 to $3,500 for a standard 200-amp residential upgrade, depending on several factors:

  • The current condition of your wiring and service entrance
  • Whether subpanels or additional circuits are needed
  • Local permit fees (which vary by municipality)
  • Accessibility of the panel location

While the upfront cost may seem significant, consider what you are getting in return: a safer home, the ability to support modern appliances and EV charging, a potential reduction in homeowner's insurance premiums, and increased resale value. Many buyers and home inspectors flag outdated panels as a condition of sale.

Is a DIY Panel Upgrade Ever an Option?

No. Electrical panel work is one of the most dangerous home improvement tasks and is illegal to perform without a license in North Carolina. The work involves direct contact with live service entrance wires that cannot be turned off without the utility company disconnecting service at the meter. Mistakes can cause fires, electrocution, and failed inspections that must be corrected at additional expense.

Beyond safety, unpermitted electrical work can void your homeowner's insurance, cause problems when selling your home, and result in fines. Always hire a licensed, insured electrician for panel work.
