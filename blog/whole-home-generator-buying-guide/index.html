Whole-Home Generator Buying Guide: What Size Does Your House Need?

With North Carolina storm seasons growing more intense, a standby generator is a smart investment for Charlotte homeowners. Here is how to choose the right size, fuel type, and installation approach.

Marcus Johnson Master Electrician, Johnson's Electric of Charlotte

Why Charlotte Homeowners Are Investing in Standby Generators

The Charlotte region has seen an increase in severe weather events in recent years, from ice storms that knock out power for days to summer thunderstorms that leave neighborhoods dark for hours. A whole-home standby generator starts automatically within seconds of a power outage and runs on natural gas or propane, so you never have to drag a portable unit out of storage or worry about fuel.

Unlike portable generators that power only a few circuits, a properly sized standby generator can run your entire home, including the HVAC system, refrigerator, medical equipment, lights, and outlets. For families with medical needs, home offices, or young children, backup power is not a luxury; it is a necessity.

How to Size a Whole-Home Generator

Generator sizing is measured in kilowatts (kW). The goal is to match the generator's output to your home's total electrical load during an outage. Here is a general breakdown by home size:

  • 10 to 14 kW: Suitable for smaller homes (under 1,500 sq ft) powering essential circuits only
  • 16 to 20 kW: Covers most needs in a 1,500 to 2,500 sq ft home, including central HVAC
  • 22 to 24 kW: Designed for larger homes or homes with higher electrical loads such as electric ranges or well pumps
  • 30 kW and above: For larger estates, multi-family properties, or homes with significant electrical demand

Calculating Your Load

The most accurate way to size a generator is with a load calculation performed by a licensed electrician. This accounts for your actual appliance draw, HVAC system tonnage, and the circuits you need to power during an outage. Johnson's Electric provides load calculations as part of every free generator estimate.

Natural Gas vs. Propane: Which Fuel Is Right for You?

Most whole-home standby generators run on either natural gas (connected to your existing utility line) or liquid propane (stored in a tank on your property).

Natural Gas

  • No fuel storage required; the utility supplies gas continuously
  • Lower fuel cost per kWh in most cases
  • Ideal for homes already connected to a natural gas line
  • Cannot run if the gas utility is disrupted (rare, but possible in major disasters)

Propane

  • Requires an on-site tank (typically 250 to 500 gallons for a whole-home generator)
  • Works even when the gas utility is down
  • Better for rural areas without natural gas service
  • Requires periodic refills and tank rental or purchase

Most Charlotte-area homeowners with existing natural gas service choose natural gas for simplicity and lower ongoing cost. Your Johnson's Electric estimator can help evaluate which makes more sense for your property.

What Does Generator Installation Involve?

Installing a whole-home standby generator is a multi-trade project that Johnson's Electric coordinates from start to finish:

  • Site assessment: We determine the best placement for the generator unit (minimum clearances from windows, doors, and gas meters apply)
  • Automatic transfer switch (ATS) installation: The ATS is the critical component that detects a power outage and switches your home to generator power automatically within 10 to 30 seconds
  • Gas line connection: We coordinate with a licensed plumber or gas contractor to extend or upsize the gas line if needed
  • Electrical connection: We connect the generator to the ATS and ensure proper grounding and bonding
  • Permit and inspection: All generator installations in the Charlotte area require a permit. We handle the entire permit process and schedule the final inspection
  • Testing and commissioning: We test the system through a full simulated power outage before considering the job complete

Installation typically takes one to two days depending on site conditions. Homeowners can expect a typical investment of $5,000 to $12,000 for a complete whole-home standby generator installation in the Charlotte metro area, including the generator unit, ATS, and all electrical and gas work.

Maintenance: What to Expect After Installation

Standby generators require periodic maintenance to remain reliable. Most manufacturers recommend:

  • Annual service including oil change, spark plug inspection, and battery check
  • A weekly automatic exercise cycle (the generator runs for a few minutes to stay conditioned)
  • Filter replacement every two to three years depending on usage
