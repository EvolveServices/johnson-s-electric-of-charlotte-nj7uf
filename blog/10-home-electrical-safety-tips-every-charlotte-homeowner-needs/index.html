10 Home Electrical Safety Tips Every Charlotte Homeowner Needs

Electrical hazards are one of the leading causes of house fires in the US. Our licensed electricians share ten essential safety practices to protect your Charlotte home and family.

Marcus Johnson Master Electrician, Johnson's Electric of Charlotte

Why Electrical Safety Should Be a Priority

According to the National Fire Protection Association, electrical failures and malfunctions are a leading cause of home structure fires in the United States. Many of these fires are preventable with simple awareness and a few targeted upgrades. Whether your Charlotte home was built in the 1970s or last year, these tips apply.

The Top 10 Electrical Safety Tips

1. Test Your GFCI Outlets Every Month

Ground fault circuit interrupter (GFCI) outlets have a Test and Reset button and protect against electrocution near water. Press the Test button monthly to confirm they trip properly, then press Reset to restore power. If they do not respond correctly, replace them immediately.

2. Never Ignore a Tripping Breaker

A breaker that trips once is doing its job. A breaker that trips repeatedly on the same circuit is telling you something is wrong, whether it is an overloaded circuit, a faulty appliance, or a wiring problem. Call an electrician rather than simply resetting it over and over.

3. Do Not Overload Extension Cords

Extension cords are meant for temporary use, not permanent wiring. Never plug multiple high-draw appliances into a single extension cord. If you find yourself relying on extension cords for everyday devices, the real solution is adding outlets where you need them.

4. Replace Two-Prong Outlets in Older Homes

Two-prong outlets are ungrounded and do not meet modern electrical codes. They offer no protection against surges and cannot support many modern appliances safely. Replacing them with grounded three-prong outlets is a relatively inexpensive upgrade that pays dividends in safety and usability.

5. Install Tamper-Resistant Outlets if You Have Young Children

Tamper-resistant (TR) outlets have a built-in shutter mechanism that prevents children from inserting objects into the slots. They have been required by the National Electrical Code in new construction since 2008, but many older homes still have standard outlets. Upgrading is simple and affordable.

6. Keep Your Electrical Panel Accessible

Your breaker box needs to be reachable in an emergency. Do not store items in front of or around the panel. The National Electrical Code requires a clear working space of at least 36 inches in front of any electrical panel.

7. Look for Warning Signs in Your Wiring

Discolored outlets, a burning smell near switches, outlets that feel warm to the touch, or sparks when plugging in a device are all warning signs of a wiring problem. Do not wait to call an electrician. These are not quirks to live with; they are fire hazards.

8. Use the Right Bulb Wattage in Fixtures

Every light fixture has a maximum wattage rating. Using a bulb with a higher wattage than the fixture is rated for can overheat the socket and surrounding wiring, creating a fire risk inside your walls. When in doubt, switch to LED bulbs, which produce far less heat at equivalent brightness.

9. Be Careful With Portable Space Heaters

Space heaters draw significant current and should always be plugged directly into a wall outlet, never into an extension cord or power strip. Keep them at least three feet from combustible materials and turn them off when leaving the room or going to sleep.

10. Schedule a Whole-Home Electrical Inspection

If your home is more than 25 years old and has never had a professional inspection, consider scheduling one. A licensed electrician can identify outdated wiring, overloaded circuits, improperly installed devices, and code violations before they become emergencies.
