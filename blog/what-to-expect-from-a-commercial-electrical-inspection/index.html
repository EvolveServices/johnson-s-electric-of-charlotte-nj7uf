What to Expect From a Commercial Electrical Inspection

Whether you are opening a new business location in Charlotte or purchasing a commercial property, understanding the electrical inspection process can save time, money, and costly surprises after closing.

Marcus Johnson Master Electrician, Johnson's Electric of Charlotte

Why Commercial Electrical Inspections Happen

Commercial electrical inspections are required in several situations:

  • New construction before a certificate of occupancy is issued
  • Major tenant improvements or build-outs
  • Re-inspections triggered by permit work
  • Due diligence inspections when purchasing commercial real estate
  • Annual inspections required for certain high-occupancy or industrial uses

In Mecklenburg County and the Charlotte metro area, inspections are conducted by local Code Enforcement. For larger commercial projects, the electrical contractor (such as Johnson's Electric) schedules inspections at defined project milestones: rough-in, service, and final.

What Inspectors Look For

Commercial inspectors work from the National Electrical Code (NEC) and any local amendments adopted by the municipality. Common inspection points include:

Panel and Service Equipment

  • Correct panel amperage and labeling for all breakers
  • Proper grounding and bonding of service equipment
  • Clearance requirements around the panel (36-inch working space minimum)
  • No double-tapped breakers (two wires on one breaker) unless the breaker is listed for that use

Wiring and Conduit

  • Correct wire gauge for each circuit's load and breaker size
  • Proper conduit fill ratios (wire not overpacked into conduit)
  • Secure and supported conduit runs at required intervals
  • No exposed wiring in occupied areas

Outlets and Devices

  • GFCI protection where required by code (kitchens, bathrooms, outdoor areas)
  • AFCI (arc fault circuit interrupter) protection for applicable circuits
  • Correct outlet spacing for commercial occupancies
  • Tamper-resistant outlets in required locations

Emergency and Egress Lighting

  • Exit signs illuminated and functioning
  • Emergency lighting with battery backup covering required egress paths
  • Photoluminescent markings where required

Common Reasons Commercial Inspections Fail

In our experience, the most common reasons commercial electrical inspections fail in the Charlotte area include:

  • Missing or incorrect panel labeling
  • Inadequate working clearance around the electrical panel
  • Incorrect conduit support spacing
  • Missing GFCI or AFCI protection on required circuits
  • Improper grounding or bonding
  • Wire gauge mismatched with breaker size
  • Open junction boxes (boxes without covers installed)

A failed inspection means scheduling a re-inspection, which delays your certificate of occupancy and your opening date. The best way to avoid this is to hire a licensed commercial electrical contractor who knows the local inspection requirements thoroughly before work begins.
